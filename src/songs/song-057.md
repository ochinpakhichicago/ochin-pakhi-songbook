[Refrain]
T: O se obhimane jay go chole
B: ও সে অভিমানে যায় গো চলে
E: He goes away in wounded pride

T: Boli mukh tule na phire chay
B: বলি মুখ তুলে না ফিরে চায়
E: I say — without lifting his face, without looking back

T: Ogo Rade dekhbi aay
B: ওগো রাধে দেখবি আয়
E: O Radha, come and see!

T: Tor kalachand chollo Mathuray
B: তোর কালাচাঁদ চলো মথুরায়
E: Your dark moon has gone to Mathura

[Verse 1]
T: Tumi kalar lagi ki na korechho
B: তুমি কালার লাগি কি না করেছো
E: What haven't you done for your Krishna

T: Roj du bela kusum phuler mala gethechho
B: রোজ দু বেলা কুসুম ফুলের মালা গেঁথেছো
E: Every day, twice over, you wove flower garlands

T: O tomar sathe mala shukaye gelo
B: ও তোমার সাথে মালা শুকায়ে গেলো
E: And with you the garland has withered

T: (Boli) Kar gole porabi Raai?
B: (বলি) কার গলে পরাবি রাই?
E: (I say) On whose neck will you place it now, Rai?

B: হায় রে ধ্বনি, ও ধ্বনি

[Glossary]
obhimane | অভিমানে | in wounded pride — the specific Bengali emotion of hurt that causes withdrawal and silence; not rage but a dignified, grief-laden turning away; Krishna goes to Mathura not in anger but in obhimaan — feeling himself unvalued or wronged
kalachand | কালাচাঁদ | the dark moon — a loving name for Krishna; "kala" = dark/black; "chand" = moon; the dark moon who is also luminous; Krishna's dark complexion described through the moon metaphor; the beautiful paradox of a dark moon
Mathura | মথুরা | Mathura — the city to which Krishna is taken away from Vrindavan; in Vaishnava tradition this departure is the great separation; after Mathura, Krishna never returns to Radha in the same way; the word "Mathura" carries the weight of irreversible loss
Raai / Rai | রাই | Radha — the intimate short name for Radha (Radharani); a term of both affection and slight pity; the narrator addresses her directly, watching her predicament with helpless tenderness
kalar lagi ki na korechho | কালার লাগি কি না করেছো | what haven't you done for Krishna — an expression of disbelief at the extent of Radha's devotion; every sacrifice, every garland, every two-times-daily preparation; the irony is that all this did not prevent his departure
roj du bela | রোজ দু বেলা | every day, twice a day — morning and evening; the twice-daily garland-weaving was Radha's act of dedicated daily worship; routine devotion made over years
kusum phuler mala | কুসুম ফুলের মালা | garland of flowers — "kusum" = flower (poetic); the fresh garland prepared to go around the beloved's neck; it withers with her (shukaye gelo) because the neck it was made for is now gone
shukaye gelo | শুকায়ে গেলো | has withered — dried and died; the garland and the garlander both wither together; the flowers were made for him, and without him they have no destination
dhwoni | ধ্বনি | sound, resonance — a sound heard but whose source cannot be seen or held; here the sound of Krishna's departure — the conch, the announcement, the city's commotion fading as he leaves; the cry "hay re dhwoni" = alas, that sound
Ayon dadar | আয়ন দাদার | Ayan Dada's — Ayan (Abhimanyu), Radha's husband in Vaishnava tradition; "dada" = an address of respect for an older man; his family lineage (kul) was white/pure before Radha's love for Krishna made it a subject of scandal
sada kule kali | সাদা কুলে কালি | a black mark on a white lineage — the social reputation of the family; "sada" = white, pure; "kali" = black, a stain; to love Krishna publicly is to shame the husband's family in the social world
ekul okul dukul | একূল ওকূল দুকূল | this shore, that shore, both shores — "ekul" = this bank (this world, worldly reputation); "okul" = that bank (the next world, spiritual standing); "dukul" = both banks; Radha has lost both — her worldly reputation and her other-worldly refuge; she has nowhere to stand
kon kule darabi | কোন কূলে দাঁড়াবি | on which shore will you stand — the devastating question; no shore left; both banks lost; she cannot go back to the family, she cannot follow Krishna; where does she stand?
Pagol Biswanath | পাগল বিশ্বনাথ | the mad Biswanath — the composer's signature; Biswanath Gosai names himself "pagol" (mad, divinely intoxicated) in the tradition of Baul and Vaishnava poets who sign themselves as fools of love; "koy choronkomole" = says, at the lotus feet = the humble speaking posture of the devotee
Kutilar Chhawle | কুটিলার ছাওলে | in Kutila's shadow — "Kutila" (the cunning one) is Ayan's sister in the Vaishnava narrative; she watches Radha's every move and reports back, creating obstacles; "chhawl" = shadow, shade, the space of influence; the mad poet's warning: don't show your anger while Kutila is lurking
gondogole | গণ্ডগোলে | trouble, commotion, mess — "gondogol" = disorder, scandal, interference; the question is sardonic: is Kutila stirring up trouble again? Of course she is.
akalete kina khaay | একালেতে কি না খায় | in this age what is not consumed — a wry, street-level observation; "akal" = this age, these days; "khaay" = is consumed, eaten; in these times everything gets swallowed by scandal and gossip; nothing is safe from being made into a drama

[Discussion.Summary]
A Vaishnava kirtan by Biswanath Gosai (who signs himself "Pagol Biswanath" — the mad Biswanath), narrated by a watching voice who speaks directly to Radha. The song opens at the devastating moment: Krishna has left for Mathura, going in obhimaan (wounded pride), without turning to look back. The narrator calls to Radha — "come and see, your dark moon has gone to Mathura." Verse 1 tallies what she gave: the twice-daily garlands of fresh flowers, years of preparation, the total expenditure of devotion. Now the garland withers with her. On whose neck will you place it, Rai? The "hay re dhwoni" refrain — alas, that sound — is the sound of departure, the fading noise of a city losing its Krishna. Verse 2 tallies what she lost: Ayan's family reputation, the white lineage now stained black by scandal. And she has lost both shores — this world and the next. Where will you stand now, Rai? The final signature verse is the mad Biswanath himself speaking from the lotus feet, with the poet's characteristic blend of tenderness and sardonic realism: don't be angry with me, O Radhe, Kutila is watching. Is that woman stirring up trouble again? Of course — in this age, what isn't consumed by scandal? The Pagol Biswanath figure — the mad poet who sees everything from his position at the feet — carries both the weight of Radha's tragedy and the wry, knowing laughter of the divinely intoxicated.

[Discussion.Lyricist]
Biswanath Gosai — a Vaishnava devotional poet/composer from the Bengal tradition, who signs himself "Pagol Biswanath" (the mad Biswanath) and places his signature "at the lotus feet" (choronkomole). "Gosai" is a Vaishnava ascetic/saint title.
