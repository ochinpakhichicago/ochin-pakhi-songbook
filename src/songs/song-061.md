[Refrain]
T: Pakhi tui pakha mele ure jabi Ajmir e
B: পাখি তুই পাখা মেলে উড়ে যাবি আজমিরে
E: O bird, spread your wings and fly to Ajmer

T: Jare ja pakhi ure ja Khwajar dorbare
B: যারে যা পাখি উড়ে যা খাজার দরবারে
E: Go, go bird, fly to the court of the Khwaja

[Verse 1]
T: Sathe koriya nio amar selam khani
B: সাথে করিয়া নিও আমার সালাম খানি
E: Take with you my greeting — my salaam

T: Bhoktir piyala bhore niye jao chokher pani
B: ভক্তির পিয়ালা ভরে নিয়ে যাও চোখের পানি
E: Take a cup filled with devotion — and take my tears

T: Sei jole dhoyaibo Khwajaro choron khani
B: সেই জলে ধোয়াইবো খাজার চরণ খানি
E: With that water wash the Khwaja's feet

T: Chum diyo chorone noyone rakho more
B: চুম দিও চরণে নয়নে রাখো মোরে
E: Kiss the feet — and keep me in his eyes

[Verse 2]
T: Chameli maloti Khwajaro gole poraiyo
B: চামেলি মালতী খাজার গলে পরাইও
E: Place a garland of jasmine and malati around the Khwaja's neck

T: Amar moner kotha gopone tare boliyo
B: আমার মনের কথা গোপনে তারে বলিও
E: Tell him the secret words of my heart, in private

T: Bhokter hiya Khwaja choron tole soNpiyo
B: ভক্তের হিয়া খাজা চরণ তলে সঁপিও
E: Lay the devotee's heart at the Khwaja's feet

T: Boliyo Khwajake koto bhalobasi tare
B: বলিও খাজাকে কতো ভালোবাসি তারে
E: Tell the Khwaja how much I love him

[Verse 3]
T: Noyone bhore eno pobitro surma boli
B: নয়নে ভরে এনো পবিত্র সুরমা বলি
E: Bring back sacred surma to fill my eyes

T: Hridoye bhore eno Khwajaro namer dhoni
B: হৃদয়ে ভরে এনো খাজার নামের ধ্বনি
E: Bring back the sound of the Khwaja's name to fill my heart

T: Sei namer modhu bhore gaibo din rojoni
B: সেই নামের মধু ভরে গাইবো দিন রজনী
E: With the honey of that name filling me I will sing day and night

T: Latif Sarkarer mon chai khwajake sarakhhon
B: লতিফ সরকারের মন চায় খাজাকে সারাক্ষণ
E: Latif Sarkar's heart longs for the Khwaja at every moment

[Glossary]
pakha mele | পাখা মেলে | spreading the wings — the full opening of the wings before flight; the gesture of complete readiness and release; the bird is sent on a journey it has never taken
Ajmir | আজমির | Ajmer — the city in Rajasthan where the dargah (shrine) of Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti stands; one of the most important Sufi pilgrimage sites in South Asia; visited by people of all faiths; a place of the Khwaja's presence even after his death
Khwaja | খাজা | Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti — the great Sufi saint of the 12th century; founder of the Chishti order in South Asia; known as "Gharib Nawaz" (helper of the poor); his dargah at Ajmer draws millions of pilgrims; the divine beloved addressed throughout the song
dorbar | দরবার | the court, the saint's gathering-place — the dargah (shrine) as a royal court; the Sufi saint as sovereign; to appear before the Khwaja is to appear at a court of divine grace
selam | সালাম | the Islamic greeting of peace — "as-salamu alaykum"; here sent as a formal and loving message through the bird; the first thing offered
bhoktir piyala | ভক্তির পিয়ালা | the cup of devotion — "bhakti" = devotion (Sanskrit); "piyala" = cup, goblet (Persian); the devotee fills a cup with devotion and tears to send to the Khwaja; tears as the liquid of sincere devotion
chokher pani | চোখের পানি | tears — literally eye-water; in Sufi poetry, tears are not weakness but the body's most honest spiritual secretion; the tears of longing are precious enough to wash a saint's feet with
choron | চরণ | the feet — "charan"; to wash and kiss the feet of the saint is the most humble form of devotion; the feet as the lowest point of the body, the point nearest the earth, where the saint's grace flows
chum diyo | চুম দিও | kiss — the act of placing lips on the feet of the beloved saint; beyond argument, beyond prayer; pure gesture of love
noyone rakho more | নয়নে রাখো মোরে | keep me in his eyes — "noyono" = eyes; the prayer to be kept within the saint's field of vision and grace; to be seen is to be protected
chameli maloti | চামেলি মালতী | jasmine and malati — two white fragrant flowers associated with devotion and the Sufi aesthetic; jasmine (chameli) = the beloved's fragrance; malati = another white night-blooming flower; a garland woven from both, placed around the saint's neck
moner kotha gopone | মনের কথা গোপনে | the secret words of the heart — "gopone" = secretly, in private; the devotee cannot come in person, so the bird must carry the innermost unspeakable content privately, as a whisper
hiya soNpiyo | হিয়া সঁপিও | lay the heart at [his] feet — "hiya" = heart; "soNpiyo" = surrender, offer completely; the ultimate act: placing the heart itself at the saint's feet, not just flowers or tears
surma | সুরমা | sacred kohl, antimony powder — "surma" (from Persian) = the black powder used to darken the eyes; surma from the Khwaja's dargah is considered holy and healing; the devotee asks the bird to bring back this sacred substance to fill their eyes — to see the world through eyes touched by the saint's presence
namer dhoni | নামের ধ্বনি | the sound of the name — the resonance of the Khwaja's name; in Sufi and Baul tradition, the name of the beloved saint or the divine is itself a vehicle of grace; to fill the heart with the sound of the name is to carry the presence home
modhu | মধু | honey — the name's sweetness; "sei namer modhu" = the honey of that name; one sings day and night intoxicated by this sweetness; the same "modhu" as in Sadher Laau — the initiatory sweetness
sarakhhon | সারাক্ষণ | at every moment, continuously — "sara" = all, whole; "khhon" = moment; Latif Sarkar's heart wants the Khwaja at all times, without interruption; the longing that has no rest

[Discussion.Summary]
A Sufi devotional song by Latif Sarkar addressed to a bird — asking it to fly to Ajmer to carry messages to Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti, the great 12th-century Sufi saint buried there. The song sends the bird with three things: a salaam, a cup of devotion filled with tears (to wash the Khwaja's feet), and a garland of jasmine and malati. It asks the bird to whisper the devotee's secret heart to the saint, to lay the heart itself at the Khwaja's feet, to kiss those feet, and to keep the devotee in the saint's sight. And it asks the bird to bring back two things: sacred surma (kohl from the dargah) for the eyes, and the resonant sound of the Khwaja's name for the heart — so the devotee can sing that name's honey day and night. The bird here functions as in the great Persian tradition of Attar's Conference of the Birds: the soul as bird, the saint's dargah as the final destination, the journey as devotion itself. What distinguishes this from purely ritual prayer is the intimacy — "gopone tare boliyo" (tell him in private, the secret words of the heart) — and the final signature of Latif Sarkar, who names himself last, as one whose heart craves the Khwaja at every moment.

[Discussion.Lyricist]
Latif Sarkar is a Bangladeshi folk-Sufi composer who also wrote Ekdin Matir Bhitore Hobe Ghor (song #92 in this book). His songs move between Baul philosophy, Sufi devotion, and the Bengali folk tradition.
