[Glossary]
manob gaari | মানব গাড়ি | the human car — "manob" = human; "gaari" = car, vehicle; the body as vehicle; the central metaphor of the song is entirely sustained: the body is a car that was made to travel to the friend's house (the divine destination) and has broken down midway
bondhur baari | বন্ধুর বাড়ি | the friend's house — "bondhu" = the divine beloved friend; in Baul tradition "bondhu" is always the divine — the moner manush, the one the soul seeks; the journey of the body is toward the divine; the car breaking down midway is the body failing before the soul reaches its destination
moddhyo pothe | মধ্যপথে | midway on the road — "moddhyo" = middle; "poth" = road, path; the car has not arrived and has not turned back; it is stuck exactly in the middle; the predicament of a life that has not yet reached its spiritual destination when the body begins to fail
Mohajone | মহাজনে | the great one — "mahajan" = the great merchant, the great creditor, the great one; here: God or the Creator, who has prepared and maintained the body-vehicle; "joton kore" = with careful attention; the Creator made the body well and filled it with fuel (life-force), but the mind-driver has not driven it wisely
tel | তেল | oil, fuel — the energy of life; the Creator has filled the tank (tanki) to the brim; the fuel is there; the vehicle's failure is not from want of fuel but from the driver's ignorance and the engine's accumulated dirt
mon driver | মন ড্রাইভার | the mind as driver — the mind (mon) is in the driver's seat of the body; it controls movement and direction; but "bhalo mondo bojhena" — the mind cannot distinguish good from bad; this is the spiritual condition: given a working car, given full fuel, but driven by a mind without discrimination
moyla jomeche | ময়লা জমেছে | dirt has accumulated — "moyla" = dirt, filth; in Baul/Sufi metaphor, accumulated impurities (perhaps karma, attachment, or simply the toxins of a life lived without awareness); the engine is dirty because the driver has not cleaned it
khoy hoyeche | ক্ষয় হয়েছে | has worn away — "khoy" = erosion, wearing down, decay; aging; the parts of the body eroding with time; not sudden breakdown but the slow wearing of use
Dynamo | ডায়নামো | the dynamo — the generator; in the body-as-car metaphor: the heart? the life-force generator? the dynamo is what generates the electricity that powers everything; when it fails ("bikol") everything dims
headlight duita jole na | হেডলাইট দুটা জ্বলে না | the two headlights won't light — the two eyes; with the dynamo (life-force) broken, the eyes go dark; the body cannot see ahead; also, once the headlights fail, driving in the dark toward an accident becomes inevitable
betikrom | বেতিক্রম | irregular, erratic, out of order — the engine is not running evenly; misfiring; the heart beating irregularly, the breath unsteady; the general condition of a failing body
break fail | ব্রেক ফেইল | brake failure — "kokhon jani" = who knows when; the brakes could go at any moment; when the brakes fail on a car with no headlights, in terrible darkness, the accident (durghotona) is certain
bishom ondhokaar | বিষম অন্ধকার | terrible darkness — "bishom" = extreme, terrible; "ondhokaar" = darkness; the darkness ahead is death; but also: the darkness of not knowing what lies beyond; and the darkness of a life driven by a blind mind
bhabna | ভাবনা | worry, contemplation — "bhabche" = is thinking; "bhabna" = the act of worried thinking; Abdul Karim sits with this worry; not panic, but the genuine concern of an old man looking at his aging body and the darkness ahead; the word also carries "bhavana" = meditative reflection

[Discussion.Summary]
Shah Abdul Karim's most famous song — and one of the great body-as-vehicle songs in the entire Bengali folk tradition. The body is a car ("manob gaari") headed for the friend's house (the divine beloved's home, the spiritual destination). It has broken down midway. The song then surveys the damage with the loving specificity of a mechanic's report: dirty engine (accumulated impurities), worn parts (aging), broken dynamo (failed life-force), dark headlights (failing eyes), irregular engine (erratic heartbeat/breath), brake failure imminent. The second verse places the blame not on the Creator (who carefully filled the tank and maintains the vehicle) but on the driver — the mind ("mon driver-e"), which cannot tell good from bad. Given a good car and full fuel, the mind-driver has brought it to this condition through ignorance and indiscrimination. The final verse is the composer himself sitting with the worry: Abdul Karim is thinking now. Ahead lies terrible darkness. He doesn't know what to do. The song's genius is its refusal of false comfort — he doesn't assure us the car will be repaired, or that the darkness ahead is fine. He sits with the honest, worried thought of an old man looking at a failing body and an unknown ahead. Shah Abdul Karim composed and performed this into his nineties, and the song only deepened as he aged. He died in 2009 at approximately 93 years old, having driven the car to its end.

[Discussion.Lyricist]
Shah Abdul Karim (1916–2009) was a folk singer and composer from Sunamganj, Sylhet district, Bangladesh. One of the great Baul-influenced folk musicians of the 20th century, he composed hundreds of songs that blend everyday rural imagery with Baul philosophy, Sufi longing, and sharp social observation. He was given the Ekushey Padak in 2001. He kept performing and composing into extreme old age — the car kept going, somehow, well past its apparent expiry.
