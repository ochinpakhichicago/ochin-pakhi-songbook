[Glossary]
nupurchhonda | নুপুরছন্দা | the rhythm of the anklet — "nupur" = anklet worn on a woman's foot whose jingling marks her movement; "chhonda" = rhythm, metre; the title fuses ornament and music into a single word
rupkowtha | রূপকথা | fairy tale, tale of beauty — "rup" = beauty/form; "kotha" = words/tale; in Bengali "rupkotha" = fairy tale; here it is both the fairy-tale world and beauty arriving quietly as a whispered story
chupisare | চুপিসারে | quietly, in secret, without sound — an adverb of gentleness; the entire emotional world of this song moves at this volume
nirobota | নীরবতা | silence, quietude — the bridge between two gazes is built not from words but from silence; silence as the deepest form of connection
sujon | সুজন | a good-hearted one, a dear person — "su" = good, "jon" = person; the refrain repeats "sujon" four times in a longing chant; at the end the chant returns but "kotha" (words) has become "byatha" (ache)
chaNd lagowa | চাঁদ লাগোয়া | nestled against the moon — "lagowa" = touching, adjacent to; the river runs so close to the moon that it brushes against it; a lover's geography
jowar bhaNta | জোয়ার ভাঁটা | the tide and the ebb — the perpetual rhythm of rising and falling; here it is the heart that moves like a tidal river
ponchoswarer | পঞ্চস্বরের | of the five notes — "poncho" = five, "swar" = note/tone; the pentatonic scale; the snare of music itself
chhilar faaNdey | ছিলার ফাঁদে | in the snare of the string — "chhila" = the string of an instrument or a bow; "faand" = trap, snare; to be caught in the string of music is to be caught in longing
byakulowta | ব্যাকুলতা | restlessness, yearning — the state of being seized by longing; it trembles in the snare of the five strings
chholat | ছলাৎ | the sound of water splashing — an onomatopoeia for water dripping or lapping against a surface; words that sound like water
rup rupoker | রূপ রূপোকের | beauty meeting the beautiful one — a lyrical reduplication; beauty and the beloved meeting in the same moment
ongo songo | অঙ্গ সঙ্গ | the body finding company — "ongo" = body, limb; "songo" = company, companionship, touch; the two rhyming words lean into each other
khonik | ক্ষণিক | for a moment, momentarily — duration compressed into an instant; the river becomes oceanic only for a flash
sagor gohin | সাগর গহিন | deep as the ocean — "sagor" = ocean; "gohin" = deep, profound; the river is momentarily fathomless
bihowlowta | বিহ্বলতা | being overwhelmed, stupefied — the state of being lost in an experience too large to hold; the river-self overwhelmed by oceanic feeling
sharom | শরম | shyness, modesty, bashful reticence — the emotion between two people when the body finds company; it repeats four times like a blush that cannot be spoken
maya-nodi | মায়া-নদী | the river of longing — "maya" = affection, attachment, illusion; the river is made of feeling rather than water
kothokawta | কথকতা | storytelling, the art of telling — the classical tradition of narrative-song; words becoming their own art form in the forgetting of time

[Arrangement]
Just Subho and drone. Harmony enters the second time through. Rhythm added on second pass of the refrain. Flute interlude after refrain. Dotara interlude with Sundarban rhythm after Verse 1.

[Discussion.Summary]
An original JitSongeet composition of lyrical density and quiet intensity. Each verse follows a three-line structure: an image from nature, the emotional consequence of that image, and a refrain line in which a single word is repeated four times like an incantation — sujon (dear one), chholat (splashing), sharom (shyness). The opening image is an anklet-rhythm crossing quietly to a fairy-tale country, a bridge of silence built eye to eye. The first verse gives the moon-touching tidal river and the snare of five strings; the second, beauty meeting beauty as the body finds company and the river briefly becomes oceanic. The outro ties everything together: near this river of longing (maya-nodi), the anklet dances, and in the forgetting of coming and going, words become pure storytelling. The final line returns "sujon" but replaces "kotha" (words) with "byatha" (ache): what began as speech has become pain felt.
