[Refrain]
T: Porer jayga porer jomin
B: পরের জায়গা পরের জমিন
E: Another's land, another's soil

T: Ghor banaiya ami roi
B: ঘর বানাইয়া আমি রই
E: And I have built a home there and live

T: Ami to sei ghorer malik noi
B: আমি তো সেই ঘরের মালিক নই
E: But I am not the owner of that home

[Verse 1]
T: Sei ghor-khana jar jomidari
B: সেই ঘর-খানা যার জমিদারি
E: The one who holds the landlordship of this house and land

T: Ami pai na tahar hukumzari
B: আমি পাই না তাহার হুকুমজারি
E: I never receive his orders or directions

T: Ami pai na jomidarer dekha
B: আমি পাই না জমিদারের দেখা
E: I cannot even see this landlord

T: Moner dukkho kare koi
B: মনের দুঃখ কারে কই
E: To whom can I tell the sorrow of my heart?

[Verse 2]
T: Jomidarer icchamoto dei na jomi chash
B: জমিদারের ইচ্ছামতো দেই না জমি চাষ
E: I do not cultivate the land as the landlord wishes

T: Taito phoshol phole na re
B: তাইতো ফসল ফলে না রে

T: Dukkho baro mas

E: And there is sorrow twelve months of the year

[Verse 3]

T: Tobu jomin amar hoy na nilam
B: তবু জমিন আমার হয় না নিলাম

[Glossary]
porer jayga porer jomin | পরের জায়গা পরের জমিন | another's land, another's soil — the body is not the soul's true property; we are tenants in a borrowed house, living on land that belongs to another
ghor banaiya | ঘর বানাইয়া | having built a home — the ego builds its identity and attachments in a body it does not own; the construction is real but the ownership is not
malik | মালিক | owner, master — the true owner of this body-home is the divine; the soul is merely a tenant passing through
jomidari | জমিদারি | landlordship, the landlord's estate — the entire body and the world it inhabits is the divine's estate; the soul holds it only on lease
hukumzari | হুকুমজারি | issuing orders, giving directions — the landlord (divine) sends no explicit commands; the tenant must figure out how to live rightly without direct instruction
jomidarer dekha | জমিদারের দেখা | seeing the landlord — the soul cannot see the divine landlord directly; that meeting is the goal of the entire spiritual life
moner dukkho | মনের দুঃখ | the sorrow of the heart — the deep existential longing to know and see the divine; the loneliness of a tenant who cannot find the owner
jomi chash | জমি চাষ | cultivating the land — the metaphor for devotional practice; one is supposed to cultivate the body-land according to the divine's will, but fails to do so
phoshol phole na | ফসল ফলে না | no harvest comes — without right cultivation (devotion and discipline), no spiritual fruit is produced; a life without harvest
dukkho baro mas | দুঃখ বারো মাস | sorrow twelve months of the year — the Bengali idiom for perpetual, unrelenting suffering; no relief through any season
khajna-pati | খাজনা-পাতি | rent and dues — all the obligations and rituals paid to the divine; even performing all the religious duties does not guarantee ownership of the self
nilam | নিলাম | auction — to formally acquire land through the landlord's auction; the speaker cannot even win ownership by paying the full price
dakhila | দাখিলা | the official receipt of payment, the tenancy document — the proof that ownership has been transferred; it never arrives; the divine does not send a receipt confirming the soul's rightful place

[Discussion.Summary]
A song by Abdul Latif using the extended metaphor of the tenant-farmer and the absent landlord to express the soul's relationship to the divine. The body is another's land; the soul builds a home there but is never the true owner. The divine landlord never issues instructions, cannot be seen, and the land cannot even be won at formal auction. The more theological point: ritual and obligation (khajna — paying the rent) are necessary but not sufficient — they do not confer ownership. The song ends with the speaker still walking, still trying to please the landlord, still waiting for the receipt that never comes. Abdul Latif was a major Bangladeshi folk composer who, like Lalon, used agricultural and everyday Bengali imagery to express the deepest spiritual longing.
