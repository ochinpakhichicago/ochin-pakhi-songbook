[Glossary]
chirodin pushlam | চিরদিন পুষলাম | all my life I kept — "chirodin" = all one's life, forever; "posha" = kept, raised, domesticated (as a pet); the shocking admission of the song: this is not an unknown bird encountered in the wild — it is a bird the seeker has kept and tended their entire life, and still does not know
ochin pakhi | অচিন পাখি | the mysterious bird — here specifically the breath (prana); the breath has been "kept" in the body all one's life, yet its nature, form, and path remain unknown; the most intimate stranger
bhed-porichoy | ভেদ-পরিচয় | secret identity — "bhed" = secret, inner distinction, esoteric knowledge; "porichoy" = introduction, identity, recognition; the bird will not introduce itself, will not reveal what it is
khede jhore aNkhi | খেদে ঝরে আঁখি | grief, the eyes pour tears — "khede" = grief, deep sorrow; "jhore" = pours, flows; "aNkhi" = eyes (poetic); the tears are not sentimental but precise: the grief of one who has kept the breath all their life and never known it
buli | বুলি | words, speech — "buli bole" = speaks its words; the breath makes a sound (the sound of breathing) that can be heard — yet the seeker cannot see the bird that makes the sound; audible but invisible
bishom ghor | বিষম ঘোর | terrible darkness, deep confusion — "bishom" = terrible, extreme; "ghor" = deep darkness, a state of profound delusion or confusion; the seeker is not enlightened — they are lost
dhulo diye dui chokhe | ধুলো দিয়ে দুই চোখে | throwing dust in both eyes — death will arrive suddenly and throw dust in the eyes before recognition can happen; the bird will fly away at the very moment the seeker finally tries to see it
posha pakhi | পোষা পাখি | the tame kept bird — "posha" = domesticated, tamed, kept in the house; the breath was never a wild creature to be caught — it was tame, domestic, always with you; and you still did not know it
chinlam na | চিনলাম না | I did not recognize — "chena" = to recognize, to know someone's face and nature; the central failure of the song; not ignorance of something distant but failure to recognize something intimately present
chinal | চিনাল | the means of recognition, the knowledge that enables knowing — "chinal" = the instrument or knowledge by which one recognizes; if I had been given this knowing-tool (by the guru, by practice), I would have recognized the bird; the distinction between "knowing the bird is here" and "having the recognition-knowledge to see it"
dhukdhuki | ধুকধুকি | the anxious beating of the heart — the heart's restless pounding from not knowing; if the breath were recognized, this anxiety would cease; to know the bird is to be at peace
noy doroja | নয় দোরজা | nine doors — the nine openings of the body (two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, mouth, and two lower openings); the same nine doors as in song #14 (Khachar Vitor Ochin Pakhi); through these doors the breath moves, but the seeker cannot determine which
kon pothe | কোন পথে | through which path — "poth" = path, road; the same question as "komne ashe jai" in song #14 but stated even more plainly: which path does the breath take?
bhelki | ভেলকি | tricks, sleight of hand, illusion — "bhelki deoa" = to trick someone's eyes; the breath is a trickster; it passes through the doors and the seeker cannot follow it with the eyes
Siraj Sai | সিরাজ সাঁই | Siraj Shah — Lalon's spiritual master (guru); "Sain/Sai" is an honorific for a Muslim mystic; Siraj Sai was the guru who initiated Lalon into the Baul tradition; his appearance in the final verse as a direct speaker is rare and powerful — the teaching is not Lalon's but the guru's, transmitted through Lalon
faNd pete | ফাঁদ পেতে | setting a trap — "faNd" = a trap or snare; "peta" = to lay out, spread; the guru's instruction is to become a hunter: set a trap
siNdh mukhi | সিঁদ মুখি | at the entrance of the burrow — "sindh" = a hole bored through a wall or the earth (specifically by a thief making a secret entry); "mukhi" = at the mouth of; the thief-burrow metaphor: the breath enters and exits the body through a small opening (the nostril), just as a thief enters through a bored hole; the guru's instruction is to watch that opening; this is a direct meditation teaching — sit at the entrance of the nostril and observe the breath as it passes

[Discussion.Summary]
A companion to Khachar Vitor Ochin Pakhi (#14), and in some ways its resolution. The question in song #14 was: "komne ashe jai" — in which direction does the mysterious bird come and go through the nine doors of the cage? This song opens by confessing that the seeker has kept the bird all their life ("chirodin pushlam") — it is not a wild stranger but a tame, domestic creature — and still cannot know it. The bird is audible (buli bole shunte pai — I can hear it speaking) but has no visible form. One day it will fly away, throwing dust in the seeker's eyes before recognition can happen. The shame of it: a kept bird, never known. Verse 3 names the tool that is missing — "chinal" — the knowledge of recognition that would have let the seeker see what was always present. Then Verse 4 returns to the nine doors, the same architecture as song #14, and the same question: through which path does the bird go? And then the guru speaks. Siraj Sai — Lalon's own master — gives the answer: "faNd pete re sindh mukhi." Set your trap at the entrance of the burrow. The burrow is the nostril; the trap is attention; the instruction is to sit at the point where the breath enters and exits the body and watch. This is the Baul meditation instruction in its most compressed form. Song #14 asked the question. Song #86 gives the guru's answer.

[Discussion.Points]
This song and Khachar Vitor Ochin Pakhi (#14) form a pair. Song #14 asks: through which direction does the bird come and go (komne ashe jai)? Song #86 asks: through which path (kon pothe)? And gives Siraj Sai's answer: watch the entrance of the burrow — the nostril.
