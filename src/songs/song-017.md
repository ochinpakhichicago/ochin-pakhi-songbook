[Glossary]
eki brinte duti kusum | একই বৃন্তে দুটি কুসুম | two flowers on one stem — "brinte" = on the stalk/stem; "duti" = two; "kusum" = flower; two flowers that share a single stem; they are distinct (separate flowers, different in name and form) but inseparable in their root; this is the image that carries the entire song
Hindu-Musalmaan | হিন্দু-মুসলমান | Hindu and Muslim — named directly, without euphemism; Nazrul wrote in a period of violent communal tension; naming them together on one stem is the political act of the poem
nayan-mani | নয়ন-মণি | the eye-jewel — "nayon" = eye; "mani" = jewel, gem; the pupil of the eye, or the beloved thing kept in the eye; the most precious thing the eye holds; the Muslim is the Hindu's eye-jewel (without the Muslim, the Hindu cannot see clearly); the Hindu is the Muslim's praan (life-breath); the two are each other's essential faculty — one for sight, one for life
Rabi Shashi | রবি শশী | the sun and the moon — "Rabi" = Ravi = the sun (Sanskrit); "Shashi" = the moon (Sanskrit, from "shasha" = the mark of a hare on the moon's face); Hindu astronomical names; in the same sky, like the sun and moon, appearing at different times or together during an eclipse, sharing the same mother-sky
maayer kole | মায়ের কোলে | in the Mother's lap — the sky as the universal mother holding both sun and moon; the common mother who holds both Hindu and Muslim as her children; this image of the divine feminine as the shared parent underpins the entire song
ek rokto | এক রক্ত | one blood — the biological fact beneath the religious distinction; one bloodline, one species; the same blood runs in the chest of both; the communal riot divides what has one blood
ek naarir taan | এক নারীর টান | one woman's pull — the pull of one mother; "taan" = pull, draw, the magnetic call; both Hindu and Muslim are pulled by the same maternal force — their country, their mother, the earth that bore them
khaayi go hawa | খাই গো হাওয়া | we eat/breathe the air — "khawa" = eating, but also a common expression for experiencing something (like "air we eat/take in"); we breathe the same air; the most basic shared condition
desher jol | দেশের জল | the country's water — the same rivers, the same rain, the same wells; the literal water of the land, shared without distinction; you cannot separate who drank from which river
maayer bokhkhye | মায়ের বক্ষে | at the mother's breast — "bokhkhyo" = chest, breast; growing from one mother's breast like the same tree's flowers; the motherland as the nursing mother
gore | গোরে | in the grave — Islamic burial; the grave is the resting place for the Muslim dead; in Islamic tradition, burial in the earth without cremation
shmashan | শ্মশানে | in the cremation ground — Hindu cremation; "shmashan" = cremation ground; the fire, the ashes returned to the river; the Hindu resting place after death
same earth | — | the final image: different rites, same soil; the grave and the cremation ground are both in the same earth, the same country's maati (soil); death makes the distinction irrelevant; the earth does not separate its two traditions

[Discussion.Summary]
One of Nazrul Islam's most famous and most necessary songs — a direct response to the communal violence of his time (1920s–40s Bengal, the period of Hindu-Muslim riots and partition anxiety). The image is exact and beautiful: two flowers on one stem. Not the same flower — two distinct ones, with their own names, forms, and traditions — but sharing one stalk. To cut one is to wound the other. To separate them is to kill both. The song then layers image on image: the Muslim as the Hindu's eye-jewel, the Hindu as the Muslim's life-breath (each the other's vital faculty); the sun and moon in one sky, rocked in the same mother's lap; one blood under one chest; one woman's pull. The second verse moves from metaphysics to ecology: the same air, the same water, the same mother's breast, the same flowers and fruit. And the final couplet moves to death: some go to the grave, some to the cremation ground — but the same earth receives them. The political argument is made through biology, through nature, through the body, through death. Nazrul refused to let religion override any of these shared facts. The song has been sung at moments of communal tension in Bengal, Bangladesh, and India ever since he wrote it.

[Discussion.Lyricist]
Kazi Nazrul Islam (1899–1976) — the "rebel poet" (bidrohi kabi) of Bengali literature; poet, novelist, musician, revolutionary, and one of the greatest lyricists in Bengali. He was of Muslim origin but married a Hindu woman, worshipped both Islamic and Hindu traditions in his poetry, and wrote songs for both. He was deeply committed to Hindu-Muslim unity and opposed the religious nationalism that was pulling Bengal apart in his time. He spent the last 34 years of his life in silence, afflicted by a neurological disease, in Bangladesh. He is the national poet of Bangladesh.
