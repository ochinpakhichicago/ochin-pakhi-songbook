[Glossary]
aarshinagar | আরশিনগর | the Mirror City — "aarshi" = mirror; a city made of mirrors; the inner realm where the divine dwells, so close yet unseen because one cannot look within
porshi | পড়শি | neighbour — the divine presence that lives right next door; the deepest paradox: what is most intimate is most unseen
geram bere ogadh pani | গ্রাম বেড়ে অগাধ পানি | fathomless water surrounds the village — the uncrossable inner barrier between the seeker and the divine; the ocean of ego and spiritual blindness
kinara | কিনারা | shore, bank — the far shore where the neighbour lives; unreachable because there is no boat
tarani | তরণী | boat, vessel — without a means of crossing, the seeker remains stranded on the wrong shore
hosto pada skondho matha nai | হস্তো পাদ স্কন্ধ মাথা নাই | no hands, feet, shoulders or head — the divine neighbour has no physical form whatsoever; utterly formless and beyond bodily description
shunnya | শূন্য | the void, emptiness — the formless realm; the neighbour dwells in and beyond the empty space of the infinite
neere | নীরে | upon the water — the divine floats in the formless, the indefinable
jom jatona | যম যাতনা | the torments of death — "jom" = Yama, the god of death; all the suffering of dying and rebirth; a single touch from the divine would dissolve it all
lokkho jojon | লক্ষ যোজন | a hundred thousand leagues — an astronomical distance; the measure of the paradox: same place, infinite gap
faNk | ফাঁক | a gap, an empty space between — not physical distance but spiritual blindness; the gap is the seeker's own lack of inner sight

[Discussion.Summary]
One of Lalon's most celebrated songs — a meditation on the paradox of the divine as the nearest and most elusive neighbour. "Aarshinagar" (the Mirror City) is the interior self; the neighbour is the divine presence living there. Yet Lalon has never seen it: the surrounding water is fathomless, there is no boat, and the neighbour is formless — no hands, no feet, no head. The distance is simultaneously infinite and zero. The song closes in the same paradox: Lalon and the neighbour occupy the same place, yet a hundred thousand leagues separate them. The barrier is not geography — it is the seeker's own blindness.
