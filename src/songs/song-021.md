[Glossary]
kotoi rongo | কতই রং | how many colors — "rongo/rang" = color, shade; but also "spectacle, drama, tamasha"; the double meaning is intentional; the world's injustices are presented as a colorful parade of dramas — amazing, bewildering, and meaningless
duniyay | দুনিয়ায় | in the world — "duniya" = world (Persian-Arabic origin, common in Bengali folk speech); the everyday world of human affairs, not the cosmic
obak bone jai | অবাক বনে যাই | I become struck dumb with astonishment — "obak" = astonished, speechless; "bone jai" = become; the astonishment is not joyful but disorienting — the world makes no sense; the observer is stunned into silence
ortho kono khuje nahi pai | অর্থ কোনো খুঁজে নাহি পাই | I cannot find any meaning — "ortho" = meaning, reason, sense; "nahi pai" = cannot get/find; the injustice of the world refuses coherent explanation; not nihilism but the honest bewilderment of a fair witness
bhalo jone bhanga ghore | ভালো জনে ভাঙা ঘরে | the good person in a broken house — the decent, honest person who lives in poverty and material ruin while the corrupt thrive; not a metaphor but literal: good people often do live in broken houses while the wicked occupy mansions
singhasone chore | সিংহাসনে চড়ে | climbs to a throne — "singhasone" = on a throne, a place of power and prestige; "chore" = climbs, ascends; the bad one rises; the world's reward system inverted
sonar fosol | সোনার ফসল | the golden harvest — "sonar" = gold, golden; "fosol" = harvest, crop; the farmer's crop described as golden — it is wealth, literally, produced by their hands; but they cannot eat from it
dui bela ahar | দুই বেলা আহার | two meals a day — the minimum standard of sustenance; two times eating; the farmer who produces the country's food cannot access two meals; the basic indignity
hirar khonir mojur | হীরার খনির মজুর | the diamond mine laborer — "hirar khoni" = diamond mine; "mojur" = daily wage laborer; the person who digs diamonds from the earth and has nothing; the starkest image in the song: the most valuable thing in the world is extracted by the most impoverished; they touch diamonds and own nothing
kanakori nai | কানাকড়ি নাই | not a farthing — "kanakori" = a blind cowrie shell, the smallest denomination in old Bengali currency; worth effectively nothing; "nai" = there is none; the laborer in the diamond mine has not even the lowest unit of currency; the absolute bottom of the economic paradox

[Discussion.Summary]
A song of social paradox in the lokgeeti form, composed by Satyajit Ray. The singer looks in every direction and sees the same thing: the world makes no sense. The good live in broken houses; the bad sit on thrones. The farmer who grows the golden harvest cannot manage two meals. The diamond mine laborer has not a farthing. The refrain's "kotoi rongo" carries both meanings — how many colors and how many dramas — turning the spectacle of social injustice into a kind of terrible theater. The observer's response is not rage but bewilderment: "I cannot find any meaning in it." This bewildered astonishment is itself the political statement. The song refuses to explain or console. It simply looks, and reports what it sees. Satyajit Ray worked extensively in this social-observation folk mode — the same clarity appears in the songs he composed for films like Hirak Rajar Deshe (1980), where injustice is exposed through the deadpan register of the folk musician, whose instrument is not accusation but accurate witness.

[Discussion.Lyricist]
Satyajit Ray (1921–1992) — film director, writer, composer, and artist. Composed music and wrote songs for many of his own films, often working in the lokgeeti and Baul-influenced folk mode. The social observation and spare, clear language of this song is characteristic of his folk-mode compositions.
