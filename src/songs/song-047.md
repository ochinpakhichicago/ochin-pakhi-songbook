[Glossary]
monete | মনেতে | into the heart — poetic/archaic locative of "mon" (heart/mind); in standard Bengali simply "mone"; the fuller form gives it a song-like intimacy
baulia sur | বাউলিয়া সুর | the Baul-flavoured melody — "baulia" is the adjectival colloquial form of Baul; a tune that carries the wandering, rootless, devotional quality of Baul music
ural | উড়াল | soaring, taking flight — the melody does not merely travel; it lifts free from the ground; the word carries the Baul's sense of liberation from the earthbound
probashi | প্রবাসী | one who lives away from home — "pro" = far from; "basi" = one who dwells; a word of enormous longing in Bengali — the friend who has left the village, the homeland, the familiar world
ektara kNapone | একতারা কাঁপনে | in the trembling of the ektara — the one-stringed instrument; its single vibrating note is enough to bind all the friends of home; simplicity as the deepest bond
ochin pakhi | অচিন পাখি | the mysterious bird, the unknown bird — the central image of Baul philosophy and the name of the band; the soul or the divine that cannot be known directly; the bird that lives within the body's cage
khacha | খাঁচা | the cage — in Baul philosophy the body is the cage in which the soul-bird lives; when the cage is "released" (chharle) or "broken" (bhangle), the bird is free; the song uses both verbs deliberately, the second more violent
praneri utsob | প্রাণের উৎসব | the festival of life — not a religious festival but the celebration of being alive; the bird's freedom is not death but full living
matir gan | মাটির গান | the song of the soil — folk music as rooted in the earth, the literal ground of Bengal; the counterpoint to the melody that soars
eker tane | একের টানে | in the pull of oneness — "ek" = one; the gravitational force of unity drawing across the multiplicity of people, places, languages, differences
bohur milonpur | বহুর মিলনপুর | the meeting-place of the many — "bohu" = many; "milon" = meeting, union; "pur" = city, place; where multiplicity becomes union
porshi bosot | পড়শী বসত করে | the neighbour makes their home — from Lalon's Barir Kachhe Aarshinagar; the divine neighbour living right next door yet never seen; here extended: across countries, the neighbour still dwells
Robir alo | রবির আলো | Robi's light — "Robi" = Rabindranath Tagore; his light is the humanist, artistic, synthesising force of Bengali culture; Lalon and Tagore as the twin pillars of Bengal's inner life
bibhed aNdhar | বিভেদ অন্ধার | the darkness of division — "bibhed" = division, difference, sectarianism; "andhar" = darkness; what Lalon's flame and Robi's light together drive back
moner manush | মনের মানুষ | the person of the heart — Lalon's central concept; the inner beloved, the divine as it lives within; to find moner manush is to find the ultimate companion
ujan | উজান | against the current, upstream — to seek is always to go upstream, against the drift of forgetting and division; music as the upstream force
mormo-pagol | মর্ম-পাগল | maddened by inner feeling — "mormo" = core meaning, inner essence; "pagol" = mad; the one so seized by depth of feeling that ordinary behaviour falls away; the precondition for the latch opening
agol | আগল | the latch, the bolt — what keeps the cage shut; when feeling is deep enough, even the latch opens; union becomes possible
dosor | দোসর | companion, partner — not any companion but the one who matches you, who accompanies you; the word suggests reciprocity and exact fit
bhangle | ভাঙলে | when [it] breaks — in Verse 1 the cage is "released" (chharle), a deliberate letting-go; in Verse 3 it "breaks" (bhangle), a shattering from within; the progression from gentle release to rupture

[Discussion.Summary]
An original JitSongeet composition that functions as a kind of manifesto for what the Ochin Pakhi Songbook is about. It traces the journey of music from heart to heart, village to city, homeland to diaspora. The ektara's single trembling note binds all the friends of home. The ochin pakhi — the mysterious bird and the band's name — appears as the image of the soul freed from the cage, first gently "released" (chharle) and in the final verse violently "broken" (bhangle): a progression from permission to rupture. In the middle, Lalon and Rabindranath appear as the twin lights that drive back the darkness of division — the Lalon reference ("porshi bosot kore") echoes the band's repertoire directly. The final verse moves inward: the sorrowful one lifts the rhythm, the voice sinks into the heart, the moner manush (Lalon's inner beloved) rises against the current in music. When feeling reaches the pitch of mormo-pagol (maddened by inner essence), even the latch opens, and the companion is found. The song moves from the social (village to city) through the communal (Lalon + Tagore, many into one) through the spiritual (moner manush) to the deeply personal (dosor — companion found). It is, unmistakably, also a song about this band.
