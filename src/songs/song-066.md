[Refrain]
T: Majhi baiya jao re
B: মাঝি বাইয়া যাও রে
E: Row on, boatman, row

T: Okul doriyar majhe
B: অকূল দরিয়ার মাঝে
E: In the midst of the shoreless river

T: Amar vanga nao re
B: আমার ভাঙা নাও রে
E: My broken boat

[Verse 1]
T: Venna kashther nouka khani
B: ভেন্না কাষ্ঠের নৌকা খানি
E: A boat made of soft venna wood

T: Majh khane tar bura
B: মাঝ খানে তার বুড়া
E: Rotting through at the middle

T: (Noukar) agar thaika pachhay gele
B: (নৌকার) আগার থাইকা পাছায় গেলে
E: If it shifts from the prow to the side

T: Golui jabe khoiya re
B: গলুই যাবে খইয়া রে
E: The prow will be lost and gone

[Verse 2]
T: Dikkha shikkha na hoite

T: Age korso biya
B: আগে করসো বিয়া

T: (Tumi) bine khote golam hoile

B: গায়ের তাকা দিয়া রে

[Glossary]
majhi | মাঝি | the boatman — the one who rows; in Bengali folk tradition the majhi is also the guide across the river of life; the boatman who rows on the shoreless water is both literal and the navigator of an impossible crossing
okul doriya | অকূল দরিয়া | the shoreless river — "okul" = without shore, boundless; "doriya" = river, sea (Persian-origin word common in Bengali folk song); a crossing with no visible bank on either side; the condition of being lost in the middle of the irreversible
vanga nao | ভাঙা নাও | the broken boat — "vanga" = broken, cracked; "nao" = boat; the vessel that is supposed to carry the seeker across is already failing; the song begins at the worst moment: midstream, in a broken boat
venna kashther nouka | বেণ্না কাঠের নৌকা | a boat of venna wood — venna (or bena) is a soft, low-quality wood unsuitable for boat-building; making a boat from it is the original mistake; the vessel was never built to last
bura | বুড়া | rotting through — "buri" = to rot from within; the middle of the boat is already decaying; not a surface damage but structural failure
golui | গলুই | the prow, the bow of the boat — the foremost point of the vessel; if the weight shifts wrong, the prow goes under; when the prow is lost the boat has no direction
dikkha shikkha | দিক্ষা শিক্ষা | initiation and learning — "dikkha" = initiation into a discipline or spiritual tradition; "shikkha" = education, learning; these should come before binding commitments; they were skipped
bine khote golam | বিনে খতে গোলাম | a slave without earning the status — "khat" = earned merit/deservingness through effort; "golam" = slave, bound servant; the person became bound without earning the right to be bound; servitude taken on without the preparation that gives it meaning
gaiter taka | গায়ের তাকা | the money of the body's labour — "gai" = body; "taka" = money; earnings from physical labor; the most honest form of income, spent without thought
bideshe bipake | বিদেশে বিপাকে | in trouble abroad, in a foreign land — "bideshe" = in a foreign country, away from home; "bipake" = in trouble, in a difficult predicament; the man who goes abroad into hardship and does not return
beta mara jay | বেটা মারা যায় | a son is lost — "beta" = son, male person; "maraa jay" = goes and is killed/lost; death or disappearance in a foreign place; the particular loss of a man in a distant land
para-porshi na janiye | পাড়া-পড়শী না জানিয়ে | without the neighbors knowing — the community does not hear; no funeral, no announcement, no mourning visible to the world; the disappearance is private
jane taro may | জানে তারো মায় | only his mother knows — "may" = mother; the final, most devastating detail: of all the people in the world, only the mother carries the knowledge of the loss; a grief that has no public face

[Discussion.Summary]
A Bhatiali-style folk song by Abbasuddin — one of the great voices of Bengali folk music. It opens with a direct cry to the boatman: row on, in the middle of the shoreless river, with a broken boat. The song then unfolds three ways a life goes wrong. First: a boat made of the wrong wood (venna — soft, unsuitable), already rotting at the middle, losing its prow. Second: marriage and obligation taken on before education or initiation — becoming a slave without earning the right to be bound, spending the body's honest earnings without wisdom. Third: the man who goes abroad into trouble and is lost — a son who dies in a foreign land, the neighbors unaware, only his mother knowing. Each verse is a different form of the same predicament: the vessel is wrong, the crossing was begun too early, and someone is now missing on the far side. Abbasuddin's genius was to hold the folk realism (the specific detail of venna wood, the golui going under, the village not knowing) and the metaphysical resonance (the shoreless river, the broken boat) in the same breath, at the same pitch.
