[Glossary]
manush bhojle | মানুষ ভজলে | worshipping/serving the human — "bhojle" = if you worship, if you honour through devotion and service; the central instruction of the song; the human being as the site of the divine, not a symbol or a text but a living presence to be actually served
shonar manush | সোনার মানুষ | the golden human — the liberated, radiant human being; not metaphorically golden but literally shining; the fully realized person; in Baul philosophy, to worship the human (moner manush, sahaj manush) is itself the path to becoming the golden human
mul harabi | মূল হারাবি | you will lose the root — "mul" = root, origin, the essential foundation; without devotion to the human, the seeker loses their ground; the root is not scripture or ritual but the living human
khyapa | খ্যাপা | the madman — Lalon's affectionate and challenging address to the seeker; one who is mad in the worldly sense (pursuing everything except the human right in front of them) or mad in the devotional sense (consumed by divine love)
mrinale | মৃণালে | in the lotus stalk — "mrinale" = at/in the lotus stalk, the stem of the lotus; in tantric-Baul physiology the central channel of the body runs like a lotus stalk; to be firm in the mrinale is to be grounded in the body's own spiritual axis
manush guru | মানুষ গুরু | the human guru — the living teacher in human form; not an abstract divine being but a specific breathing person; the Baul's guru is a human through whom the divine is made accessible; "nishttha" = steadfast devotion, deep commitment
nishttha | নিষ্ঠা | steadfast devotion, commitment — not casual respect but total, unwavering orientation toward the guru; the prerequisite for knowing
manushe manush gnatha | মানুষে মানুষ গাঁথা | humans strung through humans — "gnatha" = strung, threaded, woven; humans are not separate units but woven into each other; the divine is not elsewhere but in the weaving
aloklota | আলোকলতা | the dodder vine — a parasitic creeper (Cuscuta species) that has no roots of its own; it winds around a host tree and draws all its nourishment from it; it is often golden-yellow in colour; here the image is of humans winding through each other the way the vine winds through the tree; also: "alok" = light, "lota" = vine — a vine of light
murao matha | মুড়াও মাথা | bow the head — the act of complete surrender; to bow the head to the human is the hardest and most necessary thing; only when the head is lowered can one rise
shunyokaar | শূন্যকার | void-making, rendering everything empty — without the human, existence becomes void; the human is the only substance that makes substance out of the void; echoes both Buddhist sunyata and the Baul's practical insistence: the divine is here, in this person
manush aakar | মানুষ আকার | the form of the human — "aakar" = form, shape, embodiment; the specific bodily form of the human; not the idea of humanity but the actual form standing before you; this is what must be worshipped
tworbi | তরবি | you will cross over — "tarna" = to cross a river, to pass over to the far shore; liberation understood as crossing the river; worship the human form and you will make it across

[Discussion.Summary]
A Lalon song of compressed, urgent instruction: without the human, the seeker loses the root. Worship the human and become the golden human. The song refuses all abstraction — it is not about worshipping an idea of humanity or a divine being in human guise; it is about the actual living person, the specific embodied form (manush aakar). The "manush guru" — the human who is guru — is the doorway. "Nishttha" (steadfast devotion to this human guru) is the key. The great image of the second verse is the dodder vine (aloklota): a parasitic vine with no roots, golden in colour, winding through a host tree and drawing everything from it. Humans are strung through humans this way — drawing life, meaning, and the divine from each other. Without the human, everything is void (shunyokaar). Lalon's closing instruction (manush aakar bhojle tworbi — worship the form of the human and you will cross over) is the most radical claim in his philosophy: the crossing to liberation is not through meditation, scripture, or ritual alone — it is through attending to the specific bodily form of the human in front of you. This connects directly to Sohoj Manush (#83), which opens with the same call: try worshipping the simple human.
