[Glossary]
mataler gaan | মাতালের গান | the drunkard's song — "matal" = drunk, intoxicated; in the Baul tradition, intoxication is divine love-madness (prem-matal), not alcohol; this is the song sung by one intoxicated by the love of the divine
kokila | কোকিলা | the Indian cuckoo, the koel — a bird whose call signals spring and the ripening of mangoes; in Vaishnava and Bengali folk poetry, the kokila is the classic messenger between separated lovers; it sings without being seen; it knows the season
daal chhariyaa ural dia | ডাল ছাড়িয়া উড়াল দিয়া | leaving the branch and taking flight — the kokila has flown free; it is asked to return and settle; the bird's restlessness is the seeker's own
bondhuyar kotha | বন্ধুয়ার কথা | words of the beloved friend — "bondhuyar" = of the beloved; in Baul poetry "bondhu" is the divine beloved; the only song worth singing is the one that carries the beloved's words; every other subject is a lesser song
aam jaam | আম জাম | mango and jamun — the two fruits of the Bengali summer; when they ripen, the kokila's season has come; the invitation to descend from the upper branches and eat freely; enjoyment as part of the path
naicha naicha | নাইচা নাইচা | come down, come down — dialectal form; the bird is being invited down from the heights to enjoy what is ripe and available; do not hover; descend and eat
aaral dia boio | আড়াল দিয়া বইও | sit in hiding, sit behind cover — "aaral" = concealment, a screen; the divine song is not for all ears; the bird must sing from cover, in the manner of all true things that are known only to those who listen
para porshira gaali | পাড়া পড়শিরা গালি | the scolds of neighbors — the community that judges what it cannot understand; those who hear the divine intoxication as madness and respond with abuse; the same neighbors as in every Baul song's world
gaan dia bujaio | গান দিয়া বুঝাইও | soothe with song, make them understand through music — "bujhano" = to make someone understand, to pacify; the answer to neighborhood scorn is never argument; only song can explain what song is
maan | মান | sulking with hurt pride — specifically "obhimaan"; the particular Bengali emotion of wounded love that causes withdrawal; when the beloved sulks, the kokila is asked not to argue or cajole but to weep
chokher jol pheliyo | চোখের জল ফেলিও | shed tears — literally: let the water of the eyes fall; tears as the only adequate response to the beloved's hurt; the kokila is asked to match the beloved's sorrow with its own; music and tears as the same language
bondhuare dekha paile | বন্ধুয়ারে দেখা পাইলে | if you find/see the friend — the sender cannot search; the bird can fly where the seeker cannot go; if the beloved is found, the bird must call back immediately
Brindaban | বৃন্দাবন | Vrindavan — the sacred forest of Krishna and the gopis; the kokila's cosmic home in Vaishnava tradition; leaving Vrindavan is leaving the great holy place
kunjobon | কুঞ্জবন | the bower, the intimate grove — a secluded, vine-covered grove; more intimate than Vrindavan; specifically where Radha and Krishna meet alone; the movement from the great forest to the private bower is a narrowing from the epic to the personal
Matal Rajjak | মাতাল রাজ্জাক | the drunkard Rajjak — Dewan Rajjak, a Bangladeshi Baul-folk composer; he named himself "Matal" (the divine drunkard) in the tradition of mystic intoxication; to be drunk on divine love is to have given up all other orientations
gele maraa | গেলে মারা | gone and dead — "maraa" = dead; the composer asks the kokila to come and find him even after death; the bird outlives the singer; the song itself is the composer's presence after he is gone
amar kachhe koio | আমার কাছে কইও | come and tell me — "koio" = say, tell (dialectal); the last request: when I am dead, come to where I am and tell me if you found the friend; a request that crosses the boundary of death

[Arrangement]
Dotara and Subho start. Khol, tabla and manjira. Goes very briefly into 4.

[Discussion.Summary]
A song addressed entirely to the kokila — the Indian cuckoo, messenger and musician of Bengali longing. Each verse is a different request: settle on the branch, sing only the song that carries the beloved's words, come down and eat the ripe fruits of the season, hide from those who would see you, calm the scolds with song, weep when the beloved sulks, call out if you find the friend, leave Vrindavan for the intimate grove. And finally, the last and most extraordinary request: when Matal Rajjak is dead and gone, come and find me and tell me. The kokila outlives the composer; the bird carries the message across the boundary of death. "Matal" = the divine drunkard; Matal Rajjak (Dewan Rajjak) is a Bangladeshi folk-Baul composer who named himself for divine intoxication. The song has the compressed urgency of a season: mangoes ripen, jamuns ripen, the beloved is somewhere out there, the neighbors scold, the beloved sulks — sing now, hide if you must, weep if the beloved weeps, call if you find them. The movement from Vrindavan (the great sacred forest) to the kunjobon (the private bower) follows the same narrowing: from the cosmic to the intimate. By the end, everything has contracted to one question: did you find the friend?
