[Glossary]
dubaili | ডুবাইলি | you drowned me — dialectal form; the divine lover simultaneously drowns and floats the seeker — an image of total surrender
bhasaili | ভাসাইলি | you set me afloat — the paradox at the heart of the song: the same force that submerges also keeps one drifting
awkul doriya | অকূল দরিয়া | the shoreless sea — "a-kul" = without a shore or edge; the vast, fathomless divine or the boundlessness of existence
kul | কূল | shore, bank, resting place — a destination the seeker cannot find; the shoreless sea offers no anchor
othoi | অথৈ | fathomless, unfathomably deep — water so deep it cannot be measured
nishithe | নিশীথে | in the night
hatchhani | হাতছানি | a beckoning gesture — waving the hand to call someone; the sea/divine calls the soul with an irresistible gesture
pansha | পানশা | a river and coastal sailing vessel — used by fisherfolk on the waterways and along the coast of Bengal
sai | সাই | a large communal fishing expedition into the open sea — during peak season, groups of forty to eighty fishermen with thirty to forty boats and fifty to sixty nets would set out southward together toward the sea. Going south meant venturing into the open ocean. The leader of such an expedition was called the Saidar. (Source: "Ganga" by Samaresh Basu)
bhayla | ভেলা | raft — "jiboner bhayla" = the raft of one's life; cast upon uncertain waters with no way back
asman | আসমান | sky, heavens — from Persian/Arabic; the vast sky that mirrors the vast sea; the two eternities gazing at each other
lokhhhyo bochhor | লক্ষ বছর | a hundred thousand years — poetic measure of eternity; the sky and sea have faced each other since before time, neither having found the answer

[Discussion.Summary]
A Bhatiyali song by Jasimuddin, made famous in the voice of Abbasuddin Ahmed. The song uses the imagery of the sea voyage — the sai expedition of the East Bengal fisherfolk — as a metaphor for the soul's journey into the boundless divine. The refrain's paradox ("you drowned me, then set me afloat") captures the Sufi/folk mystic experience of annihilation in the divine (fana) and the floating that follows.

Sai: During the peak fishing season, when a group of 40 to 80 fishermen and boatmen gather together with thirty to forty boats and fifty to sixty nets to go southward for fishing, this journey is called "Sai Jaoya" (going on a Sai). Going south means venturing into the open sea. The person who leads and commands the group on this sea expedition is called the "Saidar." (Source: "Ganga" by Samaresh Basu)
