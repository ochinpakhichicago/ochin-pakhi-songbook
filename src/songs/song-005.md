[Refrain]
T: Ebar tor mora gaange baan esechhe
B: এবার তোর মরা গাঙে বান এসেছে

[Glossary]
mora gaang | মরা গাঙ | the dead river — "mora" = dead, dying; "gaang" = river (folk pronunciation of "ganga"); in the Ganges delta, old river channels gradually silt up, lose their current, and "die" — becoming stagnant backwaters; the dead river is the image of spiritual, social, or collective stagnation; a channel that once flowed has stopped
baan esechhe | বান এসেছে | the flood has come — "baan" = flood, flood tide, surge; the great seasonal surge of water that revives even silted channels; the unexpected renewal; the miracle that the dead river can flood again is the song's central surprise; a moment that will not wait
joy maa | জয় মা | Victory to the Mother — the traditional invocation of the divine Mother (Durga, Shakti, the great feminine power); the word spoken before undertaking something bold; not just a greeting but an act of surrender and courage; set the boat afloat in the name of the Mother
tori | তরী | the boat — the vessel; the same "tari/tori" as in countless Bengali folk songs; here specifically the boat that has been tied at the dock all this time, doing nothing, while the dead river lay still; now the river floods and the boat must float
maajhi | মাঝি | the boatman — not a single individual but the one who knows how to navigate; the call to the boatman is urgent: come now, today, this moment; the flood will not wait
pranpone | প্রাণপণে | with all one's life-force — "praan" = life, life-force; "pone" = with complete commitment, wagering everything; "daak de pranpone" = call with every ounce of life you have; the call must be total, not tentative
boittha ne re | বৈঠা নে রে | take up the oars — "boittha/baetha" = oar, the paddle; the oars have been lying idle; now everyone together must pick them up; the collective action — not one person but "toraa sobaai mile" = all of you together
doradori | দড়াদড়ি | all the ropes — "dora" = rope; "dori" = rope; "doradori" = all the various ropes and ties; the boat is held in place by multiple ropes; all must be undone, not just one; thoroughgoing release
dena | দেনা | debt — the accumulated debt of inaction; the obligation that has grown while nothing was done; not necessarily financial debt but the moral/karmic debt of wasted time and opportunity
becha kena | বেচাকেনা | buying and selling, trade, managing affairs — "becha" = selling; "kena" = buying; "becha kena" = commerce, transaction, the activity of a functioning life; while the river was dead, nobody was doing anything; life was suspended
kora kori | কড়াকড়ি | not a penny — "koda/kodi" = the smallest denomination (cowrie shells used as currency); the absolute minimum; "haate nai" = not in hand; there is nothing; the result of the stagnation: empty-handed
ghaate baaNdha | ঘাটে বাঁধা | tied at the dock — "ghaat" = the riverbank, the landing dock; "baaNdha" = tied; the boat has been sitting tied at the dock, going nowhere; days have passed this way; "mukh dekhabi kemon kore" = how will you face anyone?
paal tule de | পাল তুলে দে | raise the sail — "paal" = sail; "tule de" = raise it up, lift it; the sail catches the wind and commits the boat to movement; raising the sail is the point of no return; the wind takes over
baaNchi mori | বাঁচি মরি | whether we live or die — the final surrender; "baaNchi" = (if) I live; "mori" = (if) I die; it doesn't matter; commit to the crossing and let the outcome be what it is; the absolute courage of the moment when the flood has come and waiting is no longer possible

[Discussion.Summary]
A Rabindrasangeet of collective awakening and sudden decisive action. Tagore's central image is precise: the dead river (mora gaang) — the silted-up, stagnant channel in the Bengal delta that has lost its current and lies still — and then, unexpectedly, the flood arriving. "Baan esechhe" — the surge has come. The moment is now. The song is a cry: where is the boatman? Call him with everything you have. All together take up the oars. Undo every rope. Because the flood will not wait. The second verse registers the cost of the preceding stagnation: debt accumulated day by day, no one doing anything, not a penny in hand. And the third verse turns the question on the listener: the boat has been tied at the dock all this time — how will you show your face? Then the command, absolute: open it, raise the sail, whatever happens happens — whether we live or die. "Joy maa" (Victory to the Mother) frames the whole enterprise as an act of devotion and surrender to the divine force that sends the flood. Tagore wrote this in the Swadeshi period context, and it has been understood as a call to national and spiritual awakening — but its images go deeper than politics; the dead river, the tied boat, the accumulated debt of inaction, and the flood that will not wait are conditions that recur in every life and every generation.
