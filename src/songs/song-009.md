[Glossary]
praaner maanus | প্রাণের মানুষ | the person of the soul — "praan" = life, breath, the soul-animating force; "maanus" = person, human; the beloved who lives not outside but within the praan; in the Baul tradition this is the "moner manush" (person of the heart); Tagore's language is similar but his phrase moves deeper: the beloved lives in the praan, the life-breath itself
achhe praane | আছে প্রাণে | lives within the soul — the statement of fact that underpins everything; the beloved is not elsewhere; they are already present in the praan; the search outward is therefore based on a misunderstanding
heri | হেরি | I see — archaic/poetic Bengali first person, "I see"; the seeing is continuous; having recognized that the beloved is within the praan (which is the stream of light through which one sees), everything seen carries the beloved's presence
nayontara | নয়নতারা | the pupil of the eye — "nayon" = eye; "tara" = star; the pupil is the "eye-star," the central point of vision; the beloved is in this star; they are not seen by the eye — they are in the very mechanism of seeing; they are the light by which the eye sees
alok dharai | আলোক ধারায় | in the stream of light — "alok" = light; "dhara" = stream, current, flow; vision as a stream of light; the beloved is carried in this stream; they are not an object of vision but part of its movement
tai na harai | তাই না হারাই | and so they are never lost — because the beloved is in the act of seeing itself, they cannot be lost; you can only lose something external; the beloved is internal to the very structure of perception
mukher katha sunbo bole | মুখের কথা শুনবো বলে | to hear the words of their mouth — wanting to hear the beloved's actual voice, their spoken words; the most natural desire of someone who loves; but going outward to find this sound fails completely
gelam kotha | গেলাম কোথা | I went where — I went searching everywhere; the implied search was extensive, desperate; all of it futile; the voice cannot be found outside
nijer deshe phire | নিজের দেশে ফিরে | returning to one's own land — "nijer" = one's own; "deshe" = country, land, home; the return is both literal (coming home after travel) and spiritual (turning inward, coming back to oneself); the beloved's voice is heard only upon this return
tahar bani apon gane | তাহার বাণী আপন গানে | their voice in my own song — "bani" = voice, speech, message; "apon gane" = in one's own song; the beloved's voice is heard when you sing — not as something external but as something that speaks through your own creative practice; to sing is to hear the beloved speak
kangal bese | কাঙাল বেশে | dressed as a beggar — "kangal" = one in desperate need, a beggar; "bese" = dressed as, in the guise of; the seeker who goes door to door is acting out of desperation and lack; the search from a position of need does not find; the beloved is not found by the needy but by the full
dware dware | দ্বারে দ্বারে | door to door — every external threshold; temples, shrines, teachers, techniques; going outward from one door to the next; the image of the soul searching the entire external world
dekha mele na | দেখা মেলে না | cannot be found — "dekha" = sight, meeting; "mele na" = does not come; the beloved's face does not appear through external search; the method is wrong, not the intensity
amar buke | আমার বুকে | in my heart — "buk" = chest, heart; the instruction to the outward-searching seeker: come here, stop going door to door, look in this chest; the one who has found the beloved can show the seeker
dui noyane | দুই নয়নে | in my two eyes — the final instruction; the beloved who has been found is visible in the eyes of the finder; come look into my eyes and you will see them there; the eyes of the realized person carry the beloved's presence

[Arrangement]

[Discussion.Summary]
A Rabindrasangeet that speaks the Baul teaching in Tagore's own voice. The song moves in three clear stages. First: recognition — the beloved (the praaner maanus) is already within the praan (the life-breath, the soul); they are in the stream of light through the pupil; and so they are seen everywhere, in every direction, because they are not an object of vision but the light by which one sees. They are never lost. Second: the failed outward search, and the return — "to hear their words I went searching everywhere, but I could not hear"; returning to one's own land, turning inward, singing one's own song — and there: the beloved's voice, in the song itself. Third: the correction of the outward seeker — "who are you who search for them door to door dressed as a beggar? They cannot be found that way." The instruction: come here, look into my heart, look into my two eyes. The praaner maanus (person of the soul) is the same as the Baul's moner manush; Tagore was deeply influenced by the Baul tradition, and this song is the closest he came to the Baul's core teaching: the divine is within, the outward search fails, the inward return finds what was never absent.

[Discussion.Points]
Direct companion to the Baul songs in this book: Khachar Vitor Ochin Pakhi (#14), Manush Bhojle (#60), Sohoj Manush. Tagore's "praaner maanus" = Lalon's "moner manush." The method of finding — returning to one's own song, looking within — is identical.
