[Glossary]
ochin pakhi | অচিন পাখি | the mysterious bird, the unknown bird — "ochin" = unknown, unrecognised; the soul or the divine that lives within the body but cannot be grasped directly; the central image of Lalon's philosophy and the name of this band
khacha | খাঁচা | the cage — the human body; the bird (soul/divine) lives within it but comes and goes as it pleases; the cage is both home and prison, and it is made of raw bamboo — impermanent
kyamne ashe jai | কেমনে আসে যায় | how it comes and goes — the mystery of how the soul enters and leaves the body; birth and death as the bird's flight
mono beri | মনোবেড়ি | mind-shackles — "mono" = mind; "beri" = shackle, ankle-chain; the seeker wants to bind the bird with the mind itself; the instrument of capture is also the cage of capture
aat kuturi | আট কুঠুরি | eight chambers — the eight petals of the heart-lotus in tantric physiology, or the eight bodily apertures; the body as an elaborate interior palace with multiple locked rooms
noi doroja | নয় দোরজা | nine doors — the nine openings of the body (eyes, ears, nostrils, mouth, and two lower openings); the body as an architecture of gateways through which the bird moves
sodor kotha | সদর কোঠা | the main hall, the central chamber — the innermost sanctum of the body-palace; the place where the bird truly lives
aina mohol | আইনা মহল | the palace of mirrors — "aina" = mirror; the innermost chamber lined entirely with mirrors; the self seeing itself seeing itself; the divine as pure reflection; also evoking the great mirror-palace of the Mughal imagination
kopaler fer | কপালের ফের | the turning of fate — "kopal" = forehead, where fate is written at birth; "pher" = a turn, a trick; the bird's mysterious behaviour is attributed to the seeker's own fate
kacha basher | কাঁচা বাঁশের | of raw bamboo — unfinished, green, impermanent; the cage of the body is not built to last; it will one day rot and fall; this is not a threat but a release
podrbe khoshe | পড়বে খসে | will fall apart — "khoshe" = falling loose, crumbling away; the decay of the body that liberates the bird; death as the opening of the cage
Lalon keNde koy | লালন কেঁদে কয় | Lalon says, weeping — Lalon's signature with "keNde" (weeping); not grief for death but the grief of the seeker who cannot catch the bird in this lifetime; tears as the ultimate honest response

[Discussion.Summary]
The song that names the band. Among Lalon's most celebrated compositions — a meditation on the fundamental mystery: how does the soul come and go within the body, and why can the seeker not catch it? The body is described as an elaborate palace: eight chambers, nine doors, windows cut in between, a central hall, and above it all a mirror-palace (aina mohol) — the innermost sanctum where pure self-reflection is the only inhabitant. The bird moves freely through this palace but cannot be caught. Verse 3 asks: is this the bird's nature, or the seeker's fate? The bird breaks the cage and flees to the forest. The final verse turns inward with devastating gentleness: O mind, why did you cling to the cage? The cage was always raw bamboo — it was never going to hold. One day it will crumble on its own. Lalon says this, weeping: not with despair but with the honest grief of one who has spent a lifetime seeking and arrived at the most compassionate of all answers. The cage was always temporary. The bird was always free. The tears are for the time spent guarding what could not be kept.
