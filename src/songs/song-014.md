[Glossary]
ochin pakhi | অচিন পাখি | the mysterious bird — "ochin" = unknown, unrecognised; specifically: the breath, the prana; the breath comes and goes within the body without the seeker ever knowing from where it comes or where it goes; it is the most intimate thing in the body and the most unknown; also the name of this band
khacha | খাঁচা | the cage — the human body; the breath (the bird) lives within it but moves in and out as it pleases; the cage is both home and container, and it is made of raw bamboo — impermanent from the start
komne ashe jai | কমনে আসে যায় | in which direction does it come and go — "komne" = in which direction, through which path; not *how* but *which way*; the question is a precise esoteric one: through which channel does the breath move? the Baul and yogic traditions map the breath through specific interior pathways (ida, pingala, sushumna); to know the direction is to know the secret of the body; the seeker cannot determine it
monberi | মনবেড়ি | mind-shackle — "mon" = mind; "beri" = shackle, ankle-chain; if the breath could be caught, the seeker would bind it with the mind itself; the instrument of capture is also what the breath already passes through undetected
aat kuturi | আট কুঠুরি | eight chambers — the eight petals of the heart-lotus in tantric physiology, or the eight bodily apertures; the body as an elaborate interior palace with multiple locked rooms
noi doroja | নয় দোরজা | nine doors — the nine openings of the body (two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, mouth, and two lower openings); each is a doorway through which the breath-bird might pass; all are shut tight yet the bird moves freely through them
sodor kotha | সদর কোঠা | the main hall, the central chamber — the innermost sanctum of the body-palace; the place where the bird truly lives
aina mohol | আইনা মহল | the palace of mirrors — "aina" = mirror; the innermost chamber lined entirely with mirrors; the self seeing itself seeing itself; the divine as pure reflection; also evoking the great mirror-palace of the Mughal imagination
kopaler fer | কপালের ফের | the turning of fate — "kopal" = forehead, where fate is written at birth; "pher" = a turn, a trick; the bird's mysterious behaviour is attributed to the seeker's own fate
kacha basher | কাঁচা বাঁশের | of raw bamboo — unfinished, green, impermanent; the cage of the body is not built to last; it will one day rot and fall; this is not a threat but a release
podrbe khoshe | পড়বে খসে | will fall apart — "khoshe" = falling loose, crumbling away; the decay of the body that liberates the bird; death as the opening of the cage
Lalon keNde koy | লালন কেঁদে কয় | Lalon says, weeping — Lalon's signature with "keNde" (weeping); not grief for death but the grief of the seeker who cannot catch the bird in this lifetime; tears as the ultimate honest response

[Discussion.Summary]
The song that names the band. Among Lalon's most celebrated compositions — and its central subject is the breath. The "ochin pakhi" (the mysterious bird) is the prana, the breath: the thing that enters and leaves the body continuously, that is more intimate than thought, and whose direction (komne — through which pathway, in which direction) the seeker cannot determine. The opening question is precise and esoteric: not "how" but "in which direction does the breath come and go?" — because in Baul and yogic understanding, the breath moves through specific interior channels, and to know which channel is to know the secret of the body. The body itself is mapped as a palace: eight chambers, nine doors, windows between them, a central hall, and at the very top a mirror-palace (ayna mohol) — the innermost sanctum of pure self-reflection. Through all of this the breath-bird moves freely, undetected. Verse 3 asks whether this is the bird's nature or the seeker's fate — and the bird breaks the cage and flees to an unknown forest. The final verse turns on the mind with devastating gentleness: O mind, you clung to the cage — but the cage was raw bamboo from the start. One day it will crumble on its own. The breath will leave not because the seeker failed to catch it, but because the container was always temporary. Lalon says this, weeping: not from despair but from the honest grief of one who spent a lifetime trying to know the direction of the breath, and found at the end that the cage itself was the only thing that was ever knowable — and it was already falling apart.
