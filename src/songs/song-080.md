[Glossary]
sure o banir mala | সুরে ও বাণীর মালা | the garland of melody and words — "sur" = melody, musical note; "bani" = words, speech, utterance; "mala" = garland, necklace; the beloved's first act was to adorn the speaker with music and language — the garland of art as the first touch
chhuniyachhile | ছুঁইয়াছিলে | you touched — the past perfect: the touching happened once and completely; all that follows is the aftermath of that first contact
anurag-kumkum | অনুরাগ-কুমকুম | desire's vermilion — "anurag" = longing, passionate attachment; "kumkum" = the red vermilion powder; applied to the body and mind like sindoor, yet without the deeper commitment of the heart; arousal without love
banshi | বাঁশি | the flute — in the Bengali/Sanskrit tradition the flute is inseparable from Krishna; the one who plays and hides is always also Krishna playing for Radha; Nazrul invokes this without naming it
phul fotale | ফুল ফোটালে | you made the flower bloom — the beloved caused the opening of something in the speaker, then abandoned it; a flower opened in the wrong season withers
hay hay | হায় হায় | the cry of anguish — an exclamation of loss; the heart that has lost something it cannot name can only repeat this sound
kere nahi nile | কেড়ে নাহি নিলে | you did not take it away — the speaker's paradox: if you touched me and left, why not take everything and be done? The wound is the incompleteness of the taking
jaraye dhoriya | জড়ায়ে ধরিয়া | having embraced — the physical embrace that preceded the withdrawal; the hurt is precisely that the embrace was real
obhimaan | অভিমান | hurt pride, sulking from love — not anger but the particular Bengali emotional register of withdrawal from a loved one due to feeling hurt or unappreciated; it is a form of love, not rejection; Krishna's "obhimaan" toward Radha is a classical trope
madhuri | মাধুরী | sweetness, loveliness — the particular quality of feeling that existed in the moment of contact and has since gone quiet
ras-ananda | রস-আনন্দ | the joy of rasa — "rasa" = aesthetic/devotional flavour (the Sanskrit rasa theory); "ananda" = bliss; together: the state of full aesthetic-devotional joy; what the speaker had briefly and has now lost
prem-mukul | প্রেম-মুকুল | the love-bud — the unopened flower of love; the beloved came to help it bloom (fotate) but left before it could fully open
bhul bhangayile | ভুল ভাঙাইলে | to break open the misunderstanding — "bhul" = mistake, misunderstanding; "bhangano" = to break, shatter; the speaker's final insight: the blow (aghat) was perhaps exactly what was needed to shatter the false understanding — but it was not followed through; the wound was struck without the healing

[Discussion.Summary]
A Nazrul song of exquisite ambivalence — addressed to a beloved who touched without fully loving, who played the flute and hid, who embraced and then withdrew. The speaker moves through bewilderment (why give desire but not love?), longing (where did you go?), and finally a disorienting insight: you came to make the love-bud bloom; I misunderstood you; you struck a blow that should have shattered my misunderstanding — but you didn't follow through. The song can be read as romantic, divine, or self-addressed. The Krishnaite echoes — the flute, the hiding, the flower blooming then withering — place it in the tradition of Radha's complaint. But Nazrul's language is more personal and more confused than classical complaint: the speaker does not know what was wanted (ki cheyechhile), does not know why the embrace ended in withdrawal (kon obhimaane), and ultimately takes responsibility (bujhechhinu ami bhul) while still asking why the wound was not completed. The song ends not in resolution but in the question that most love poetry avoids: why was the blow struck, and why, having struck it, did you not finish what you began?
